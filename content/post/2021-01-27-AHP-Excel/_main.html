Spreadsheet software is broadly accessible

Although most data scientists and statisticians might groan at the prospect of data automation and analysis using popular spreadsheet platform, such as Excel, Google Sheets and others, the popularity of spreadsheet tools is indisputable. The popularity stems in part from its

  • accessibility to non-data scientists and non-programmers;

  • availability on most platforms including Windows, iOS, Android and cloud servers;

  • portability–the same spreadsheet can be run on independently on a laptop or launched on the internet;

  • cell formulas, many of which are intuitive for basic math needs; and

  • support from a large online community of spreadsheet users, consultants and software vendors.

A full-featured application, such as ExcelTM, can also bridge many organizational needs from simple maintenance of contacts to elaborate dashboards and fully automated data crunching (e.g., TheSmallman.com). Given the wide utility of (programmable) spreadsheets, a motivated beginner in spreadsheet technology can expect to find both room for growth and ample support developing deeper expertise using such tools.

Given all the positives, why are data scientists, engineers and other “quants” so negative about spreadsheet tools? First, comparing the spreadsheet to complete statistical platforms, such as SAS, Tableau, MiniTab, and many others, is an apples-and-oranges exercise! Statisticians, operations research engineers and data scientists will easily dismiss Excel by comparison to sophisticated statistical packages. Although some spreadsheet software have an impressive array of built-in and add-in statistical and mathematical functions, those platforms are far from being pre-programmed for descriptive, inferential and other statistical modeling. Second, although rigorous quantitative solutions for business analysis and forecasting can be programmed in Visual Basic for Applications, C# or C++, Javascript, or Typescript, the compilation and deployment tools can be bewildering to the typical spreadsheet user. Third, programming modules are difficult to manage in an organization and, (albeit in a shrinking number of cases), unsafe to share across the internet. Finally, spreadsheet programs typically run in a size-limited container making them unwieldy or even useless for a “big data” project.

Comparing Spreadsheets to R and Python

Perhaps the two most popular tools for data science are the open-source languages R and Python. R is a statistical language program that is both free and easy to learn. Python is a free programming language that has a significant set of packages for data analysis and scientific computing. Both programs can “run anywhere” and they can be used with user-friendly integrated development environments (IDEs) such as RStudio for R and Idle for python. For example, this blog and website are developed in RStudio using the R package, blogdown.

In spite of apparent unpopularity for statistical programming and data chores, desktop spreadsheet software remains popular due to its broad accessibility to users. A Basic Decision Matrix

We will develop a simple decision suitable as a foundation to multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA). Decision matrices are a basic and intuitive approach to choosing one alternative among many based the performance of the alternative across the criteria. The matrix approach is useful for a range of simple decisions from personal to corporate choices. We will begin with a common decision of choosing a new car.

For example, I might want to choose a new car based on some criteria, including cost, safety, capacity, fuel economy and style. Although there are possibly many more criteria that can be identified, an exhaustive list is not necessary for this example nor is it even helpful.1 The simplest way to organize the car choice example is to create a table having columns of the criteria and rows of cars (alternatives).
